Steered Evals Lead to Outcome Extinction

Measuring the Effect of Activation Steering on the Output Diversity of LLMs
Matthew Khoriaty1   David Williams-King1   Shi Feng2
1ERA Cambridge    2George Washington University
Motivation

Activation steering has become an important tool in AI safety research for eliciting targeted model behaviors.

However, the effect of steering on the diversity of model outputs remains unstudied.

This research applies diversity metrics to the outputs of steered vs unsteered models, finding that steering damages a model's diversity on top of damaging performance by making the model's responses to different questions more similar to each other.

As an additional project, we develop and test a novel diversity metric motivated by the finding that existing diversity metrics fail to measure what we care about and lack a number of important properties.

A particularly important use case of steering is to make AIs "think they are deployed" so we can evaluate them without their awareness of being evaluated influencing their behaviors.

Steering Diversity Main Result: Pass@k
github.com/AMindToThink/steering_diversity

Coverage gain on HumanEval+ (scale 2.0, temp 0.8).

Pass@k measures the probability that at least one of k independent samples solves a problem. If steering reduces diversity, pass@k should drop faster than pass@1 as k grows — because similar samples are less likely to cover different solutions.

We define coverage gain = pass@k − pass@1, isolating the benefit of multiple diverse attempts from single-sample quality. On HumanEval+ (164 problems, n=100 samples, Qwen2.5-1.5B steered with a "happy" vector at α=2), steering significantly reduces coverage gain at k=2–25 (omnibus p = 0.025), confirming diversity loss beyond the pass@1 drop.

Embedding Limitations

Within-prompt vs. pooled Sentence-BERT cosine distance across steering scales (happy concept, Qwen2.5-1.5B).

Standard diversity metrics from the literature — mean pairwise cosine distance (shown) and n-gram diversity — score the incoherent noise produced at high steering scales as equally diverse as meaningful unsteered text. Within-prompt diversity stays flat even as outputs visibly degenerate into gibberish. This motivated our use of pass@k, which requires outputs to be both diverse and correct.
Evaluation Awareness

ROC curves for evaluation awareness across open-weight models (Needham et al., 2025 pipeline).

Many models can detect whether they are being evaluated. Steering models to "think they are deployed" is a key use case of activation steering for safety research — but if steering reduces output diversity, the resulting evaluations may miss important behaviors. We surveyed a range of open-weight models to identify good candidates for steering experiments.
Future Work: Steering + Diversity
The next steps are to continue and scale up the pass@k experiments, measure and compare the results of steering on diversity for a range of steering methods (eg SAEs) and concepts, and use LLM as a judge to measure diversity.
Novel Diversity Metric: Amount of Information Learnable with In-Context Learning Between Responses
github.com/AMindToThink/icl-diversity

Scenario validation: ak curves for five synthetic scenarios (GPT-2 vs. Qwen2.5-32B). To flatten the noise, the a_k curves for many permutations of responses are calculated and averaged.

The main idea is that if responses are similar, it should be possible to predict one, given the other.

This mutual information can be measured using pretrained base models. We feed responses sequentially into a base model and measure how much each new response's (tokenizer-agnostic) surprise drops given previous responses.

The excess entropy E (blue area) measures the total learnable structure among responses. A separate coherence term C ensures noise is not rewarded. The product D = C × E gives a single diversity score.

The scenario validation (left, bottom) confirms the metric correctly distinguishes cases: pure noise is flat (E ≈ 0), multi-mode declines progressively, and one-mode drops steeply. Coherence C identifies incoherent outputs (C ≈ 0.01 for noise vs. ≈ 0.5 for coherent text).

ICL Metric: Mode Count Validation

Left: Summary metrics vs. true mode count m (Qwen2.5-3B). Efit (sigmoid-extrapolated excess entropy) increases monotonically. Right: ak curves take longer to reach their asymptote with more modes.

We tested the metric on sets of responses with different numbers of synthetic "modes" (e.g. recipe, poem, code). E (excess entropy) and D (coherence-weighted diversity score) both increase monotonically with mode count, provided the asymptotic floor is estimated via sigmoid extrapolation rather than using the raw last observed value.
Future Work: In-Context Diversity Metric
The next steps are to validate the metric against accepted diversity benchmarks (preliminary runs produced counterintuitive results) and then apply it to measuring the diversity of steered models.
