\section*{Reading Guide}
\label{sec:walk-guide}

This document develops a theoretical account of why activation steering
collapses the diversity of a transformer's outputs, and tests the
account empirically on two open-weight models. It is written so a reader
with no prior exposure to the project can follow it linearly. The
structure is:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{First-order theory} (Section~\ref{sec:diversity-reduction}).
    Taylor-expand the RMSNorm projection around the steered mean. Result: an
    explicit formula for the post-normalization covariance, with two terms ---
    a rank-reducing projection orthogonal to the steered mean direction, and a
    variance-shrinking factor of $1/\|\mu+s\|^2$.
  \item \textbf{Global geometric bounds} (Section~\ref{sec:geometric-bound}).
    Use only an elementary chordal-contraction lemma to derive distribution-free
    upper bounds on spherical variance, expected pairwise distance, and
    spherical diameter. These bounds are exact, not approximations.
  \item \textbf{Second-order corrections} (Section~\ref{sec:second-order}).
    Refine the Taylor expansion to second order. Two new effects appear: sphere
    curvature pulls the mean resultant length below $1$, and a radial-perpendicular
    coupling encodes the magnifying-glass effect near the origin. The refined
    formula is what predicts the parabolic shape we observe in the empirical
    $V(s)$ curves.
  \item \textbf{When the bounds bite} (Section~\ref{sec:conditions}).
    Identify the algebraic condition $\|\mu+s\| > \|\mu\|$ under which the
    bounds correspond to a genuine reduction relative to the unsteered baseline,
    discuss what happens when it fails (the anti-pole regime), and argue that
    it is generically satisfied in practice.
  \item \textbf{Synthesis} (Section~\ref{sec:synthesis}).
    Reconcile the local and global analyses, show they agree on scaling, and
    enumerate the testable empirical predictions.
  \item \textbf{Experiments} (Section~\ref{sec:empirical-verification}).
    Test all nine claims on Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct and Llama-3-8B-Instruct, with
    three trained EasySteer vectors and two random-direction controls. The
    headline finding: the scaling law is a statement about \emph{aggregate
    steering magnitude}, not semantic direction; random vectors with matched
    aggregate magnitude reproduce trained-vector behaviour on Llama
    (slope $V = -1.85$ vs $-1.92$).
  \item \textbf{Second-order consistency check} (Section~\ref{sec:walk-second-order-check}).
    The parabolic shape of $V(s)$ on log--log axes is exactly the second-order
    prediction: $V(s) \propto 1/\|m(s)\|^2$ with $\|m(s)\|^2$ a quadratic in
    $s$. Fitting the quadratic recovers $\|\mu\|$ to within $\pm 10\%$ across
    all runs.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent
Proofs are interleaved with the statements throughout; readers focused on
empirical claims may safely skim Sections~\ref{sec:diversity-reduction}--\ref{sec:second-order}
for the formulas in shaded boxes and proceed directly to
Section~\ref{sec:empirical-verification}. We refer to the nine quantitative
predictions as ``Claim 1'' through ``Claim 9''; the mapping between claim
numbers and theorems is summarized in the empirical section's headline
table.

\subsection{Second-Order Consistency Check: The Parabola Has a Meaning}
\label{sec:walk-second-order-check}

The log--log plots of $V(s)$ in the scale sweeps of
Section~\ref{sec:empirical-scaling} are not straight lines. They are smooth
curves with a knee --- ``parabola-like'' on log--log axes. The first-order
slope-$-1$ prediction of Claim 8 captures the asymptotic tail but not the
shape of the bend. We check directly whether the second-order theory of
Section~\ref{sec:second-order} accounts for it.

From the second-order spherical-variance formula
\eqref{eq:spherical-variance-taylor},
\begin{equation}
  V(s) \;\approx\; \frac{\operatorname{tr}\Sigma_x - \hat{m}^\top \Sigma_x \hat{m}}{2\,\|m(s)\|^2}.
  \label{eq:walk-V-second-order-recap}
\end{equation}
Substituting $m(s) = \mu + s\,v_{\mathrm{eff}}$, the denominator is a strict
algebraic quadratic in $s$:
\begin{equation}
  \|m(s)\|^2
  \;=\; s^2\,\|v_{\mathrm{eff}}\|^2
  \;+\; 2 s\,(v_{\mathrm{eff}} \cdot \mu)
  \;+\; \|\mu\|^2.
  \label{eq:walk-m-quadratic}
\end{equation}
This makes two falsifiable predictions:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Quadratic intercept.} Fitting $\|m(s)\|^2 = a s^2 + b s + c$
    to the measured $\|m(s)\|^2$ at $s > 0$ and \emph{extrapolating to
    $s = 0$} must recover $\sqrt{c} = \|\mu\|$ measured directly at the
    unsteered scale. The independently measured $\|\mu\|$ is the norm of
    the raw mean residual at $s = 0$ (available from \texttt{stats.pt});
    no fitting is involved.
  \item \textbf{Asymptotic constant.} In the deep-asymptotic regime where
    the second-order Taylor expansion has converged, the product
    $V(s) \cdot \|m(s)\|^2$ should equal
    $(\operatorname{tr}\Sigma_x - \hat{m}^\top \Sigma_x \hat{m})/2$.
\end{enumerate}

Both predictions are tested by
\texttt{scripts/bounds/second\_order\_consistency\_check.py}, which extracts
$(s, V, \|m\|, \operatorname{tr}\Sigma_x, \hat{m}^\top \Sigma_x \hat{m})$
from each \texttt{stats.pt} file in \texttt{outputs/bounds/} and writes the
results to \texttt{outputs/bounds/second\_order\_check/}.

The intercept-recovery test passes cleanly. \texttt{qwen\_random} matches
$\|\mu\|$ to four decimal places. The shape of the $V(s)$ log--log curve
\emph{is} the quadratic structure of $\|m(s)\|^2$, and that quadratic bottoms
out at $\|\mu\|^2$ --- which is the unsteered residual mean norm at the
measurement site, recovered without any prior knowledge from a fit on
$s > 0$ data.

\paragraph{Asymptotic constant (Prediction 2).}
The ratio $V_{\mathrm{predicted}} / V_{\mathrm{measured}}$ should approach
$1$ as the Taylor expansion converges. On Llama, this ratio drops from
$\approx 4$ at $s = 0.5$ to $0.97$ at $s = 8$ for the trained creativity
vector and to $0.96$ for the aggregate-matched random control --- the
asymptotic prediction is quantitatively correct in the high-$s$ tail of
both runs. On Qwen, the ratio plateaus at approximately $1.5$ across all
scales, because Qwen's natural residual-stream norm is large enough that
even $s = 8$ does not push $\|m(s)\|^2$ deep into the asymptotic regime.
This is the same finding as Claim 8's failure to manifest on Qwen, restated
through the second-order lens: \emph{the parabola does not flatten into a
slope-$-1$ asymptote within the scales the model can tolerate}.

\paragraph{What this changes about Claim 8.}
The first-order slope-$-1$ prediction of Claim 8 is the asymptote of the
second-order curve; it is not the curve itself. The deviation from a clean
slope-$-1$ at moderate $s$ is not a failure of the theory but its prediction:
$V(s) \propto 1/\|m(s)\|^2 = 1/(a s^2 + b s + c)$, which on log--log axes
crosses smoothly from a flat shelf at $s \ll \|\mu\|/\|v_{\mathrm{eff}}\|$
to slope $-2$ at $s \gg \|\mu\|/\|v_{\mathrm{eff}}\|$. The slope-$-1$
behaviour for $V$ (rather than $-2$) emerges only after combining this with
the chordal-vs-spherical-variance scaling correspondence of
Section~\ref{sec:synthesis}; both predictions agree once the regime is deep
enough.

In summary, the second-order theory survives an independent quantitative
test that the first-order theory cannot make: it predicts the
\emph{shape} of $V(s)$, not just its asymptote, and that prediction lets
us recover the unsteered $\|\mu\|$ from the curvature of $V(s)$ alone.
